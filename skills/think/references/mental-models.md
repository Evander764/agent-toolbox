# Mental Models Library

This is a compact working set, not a decorative list. Use only models that
change the diagnosis, decision, or experiment.

## Non-negotiable Lenses

- First principles: reduce the issue to durable constraints instead of inherited
  labels, analogies, or social proof.
- Entropy: every system drifts toward disorder unless energy, feedback,
  maintenance, and boundaries counteract it.
- Inversion: ask how this fails, how to destroy the goal, or what would make the
  opposite result inevitable.

## Core Munger-style Models

- Incentives: show who is rewarded for which behavior; never trust stated
  motives without incentive analysis.
- Opportunity cost: every option spends attention, capital, time, reputation, or
  optionality that cannot be spent elsewhere.
- Circle of competence: know where judgment is strong, weak, or borrowed.
- Margin of safety: leave room for bad estimates, delay, variance, and human
  error.
- Base rates: compare against similar cases before trusting the inside view.
- Second-order effects: ask what happens after the first obvious effect.
- Feedback loops: identify reinforcing and balancing loops.
- Compounding: small repeated gains or costs dominate one-time wins over long
  horizons.
- Bottlenecks: system output is constrained by the narrowest active constraint.
- Scarcity: value often follows what is hard to produce, coordinate, trust, or
  sustain.
- Comparative advantage: do the work where relative advantage is highest, not
  where absolute ability merely exists.
- Optionality: prefer moves that keep valuable future paths open when
  uncertainty is high.
- Path dependence: early choices create switching costs and default futures.
- Regression to the mean: unusually good or bad outcomes often normalize.
- Power laws: a small number of variables, users, channels, or decisions may
  dominate results.
- Redundancy: critical systems need backups; excessive redundancy adds cost and
  complexity.
- Game theory: account for other actors adapting after seeing the move.
- Principal-agent problem: the actor doing the work may not optimize for the
  principal's real goal.
- Signaling: some actions exist to create perception, not direct utility.
- Psychological denial: people often protect identity, sunk costs, or comfort
  before truth.
- Confirmation bias: evidence is filtered to preserve the preferred story.
- Availability bias: recent, vivid, or loud evidence can overpower base rates.
- Social proof: popularity is evidence of attention, not necessarily truth.
- Sunk cost: past investment is not a reason to continue a bad path.
- Survivorship bias: visible winners hide the failed denominator.

## Use Pattern

For each selected model, write one line:

```text
Model -> what it changes -> implication -> experiment or evidence needed
```

Discard models that do not change anything.
